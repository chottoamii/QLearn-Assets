def get_result_from_backend(qc, shots=1000, simulator_name='qasm_simulator'):
    
    # Choose the backend (simulator or real quantum device)
    backend = Aer.get_backend(simulator_name)

    # Transpile the quantum circuit for the chosen backend
    transpiled_qc = transpile(qc, backend)
    
    # Execute the quantum circuit on the chosen backend
    result = execute(transpiled_qc, backend, shots=shots).result()
    
    return result

def get_unitary_matrix(qc):
    result = get_result_from_backend(qc,simulator_name='unitary_simulator')
    return result.get_unitary(qc, decimals=3)

Demonstration with a Simple Circuit¶

Recall that, P-gate simply rotates the state-vector by a given angle around the Z-axis. Therefore, it can be better visulaized through the state vector.

Again, to observe the effect of rotating around the Z-axis, we should first do some preprocessing. Because the states |0> and |1> both lie on the Z-axis.

The Matrix¶

Since P-gate takes the rotation angle as an additional parameter, the matrix will obviously depend upon this angle. But let's check it for some specific angles.

In [29]:
qc = QuantumCircuit(1)

qc.p((math.pi/6), 0)
    
print(get_unitary_matrix(qc))

Operator([[1.   +0.j , 0.   +0.j ],
          [0.   +0.j , 0.866+0.5j]],
         input_dims=(2,), output_dims=(2,))

In [31]:
qc = QuantumCircuit(1)

qc.p((math.pi/2), 0)
    
print(get_unitary_matrix(qc))

Operator([[1.+0.j, 0.+0.j],
          [0.+0.j, 0.+1.j]],
         input_dims=(2,), output_dims=(2,))

As expected, the first column never changes, implyting that the coefficient of |0> stays the same. But the coefficent of |1> gets rotated by the given angle.
